How to Export Apple Health Data to CSV (Including Apple Watch Data)

Apple Health export usually gives you a large XML file, not a clean spreadsheet. This guide explains the Apple Health export process, what the XML contains, and how to get to a CSV-friendly workflow without wasting hours on cleanup.

Published Feb 22, 2026 • Coach-friendly workflow guide

If you found this page because Apple Health export gave you a large XML file, you are not alone.

Quick answer

Apple Health exports data as an XML file (often inside a zip). To get CSV, you usually need a converter or a Mac analysis tool that can import the Apple Health export and export or visualize the metrics you actually need. If you are a coach, the fastest path is often to import the export on Mac and generate a report directly instead of rebuilding everything in spreadsheets.

What is included in an Apple Health export?

An Apple Health export can include records, workouts, and other entries collected by iPhone, Apple Watch, and apps that write to Apple Health. The exact fields vary by device, permissions, and what a client has enabled.

  • Records for metrics such as steps and heart-rate-related measurements
  • Workout data and timestamps
  • Metadata and source information
  • export.xml inside a compressed archive in many cases

How to export Apple Health data from iPhone

  1. Open the Health app on iPhone.
  2. Tap your profile picture in the top-right corner.
  3. Scroll down and choose Export All Health Data.
  4. Confirm export and save or share the file.
  5. Transfer the exported file to your Mac for analysis.
Tip: Keep a naming pattern like client-name_YYYY-MM-DD_health-export.zip. It makes recurring weekly imports much easier.

Why Apple Health export to CSV is harder than people expect

The main issue is not data access. It is format and scale. XML is portable, but most people searching for “Apple Health CSV” actually want one of these outcomes: a clean table for a few metrics, a weekly trend summary, or a report they can send to someone else.

  • XML is not convenient to inspect manually
  • Different metrics matter for different goals
  • Missing or incomplete fields can produce confusing tables
  • Spreadsheets become expensive in time when repeated every week

3 ways to work with Apple Health data in CSV-friendly form

Option 1: Manual XML conversion to CSV

You can parse export.xml and convert it into CSV tables. This gives maximum control but is usually the slowest option if your real goal is recurring coaching reports.

Option 2: Export utility apps

Some tools focus on converting Apple Health exports into CSV or JSON. These reduce technical effort, but you may still end up doing analysis and report formatting manually in Excel.

Option 3: Mac analysis workflow (recommended for coaches)

If the real goal is weekly coaching decisions, use a Mac workflow that imports Apple Health exports, shows metric completeness, explains trend changes, and generates reports directly. This removes most spreadsheet cleanup while preserving portability controls.

What to check before you trust a CSV or report

No matter which workflow you use, check completeness before interpreting trends. Missing sleep or HRV data can make a score or chart look more dramatic than it really is.

  • Export date and covered range
  • Which metrics are actually present
  • Missing-data handling (labeled vs silently zeroed)
  • Units and consistency
  • Whether trend baselines are explained
